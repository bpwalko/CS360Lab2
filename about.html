Hello, in case you didn't see on the home page my name is Robert Walko, I'm a Computer Science undergraduate student at the University of Idaho: University of Idaho Home Page My primary focus is in Cybersecurity, I've taken classes on basic cyber principles, Information Assurance, Digital Forensics, Penetration Testing, and Network Security. I have a standard CS background as well, I'm familiar with most common programming languages, with Python and C/C++ where I have the most experience. I've used database systems both in school for our Database Systems class, and in research projects. I am also a recipient of the NSF Scholarship for Service, where NSF pays for our education on the condition that we work six months for a federal executive agency for every semester they cover. This program prepares students for a career including their written and spoken communication abilities, interview skills, ethics as it relates to information technology, and ability to prepare and deliver quality presentations. I have also led teams in two cyber security competitions, one was the Spokane 4th Annual Mayor's Cybercup in which we placed 1st out of 18 teams, and the NCAE cybergames 2023 in which we placed 2nd out of 12 teams.

I've completed two summer internships, one at the University of Idaho and one at the Institute for Defense Analyses. At the University of Idaho, I set up secure environments for cybersecurity labs, I became familiar with Faronics Deep Freeze, VMware Workstation, Clonezilla, and Microsoft SCCM now known as Microsoft Endpoint Manager (MEM). At the institute for Defense Analyses, I worked on a software fuzzing tool that was designed to compare usable code with formal models. The first thing I implemented was coverage statistics using the Gnu Compiler Collection tool gcov. This showed how much of the program had been reached by the fuzzing inputs we generated. The next thing I implemented was a tool to help identify where errors were in the code. The last thing I did was modify the fuzzing tool such that it could accept formal models as a ground truth. Initially it took known good C code and compared it to artifically flawed C code. I made it such that it could accept and run NuSMV formal models as the known good, such that it could run on an actual program we were developing and wanted to test.
